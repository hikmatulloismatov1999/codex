\title{Almost Continuity and the Translation Condition in the Kolmogorov--Riesz Theorem}
\author{Ismatoh}
\date{\today}

\begin{abstract}
The Kolmogorov--Riesz compactness theorem characterizes relatively compact
families in $\Lp(\R^n)$ by three conditions: boundedness, tightness, and
uniform control of translations. This paper explains the translation condition
as a form of almost continuity for a family of functions. After presenting the
theorem, we show how almost continuity enters the proof through mollification
and finite-dimensional approximation on compact sets. The main point is that
translation control converts weak information about size and support into
strong compactness in the $\Lp$ norm.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Compactness is one of the central tools in analysis. It allows one to pass from
approximating sequences to actual limiting objects, and it is especially useful
when direct formulas for limits are unavailable. In spaces of continuous
functions, the Arzela--Ascoli theorem gives a familiar compactness principle:
uniform boundedness and equicontinuity imply compactness on compact domains.

For $\Lp$ spaces the situation is subtler. Functions in $\Lp$ are equivalence
classes, so pointwise continuity is not the natural language. Instead,
continuity is measured by translations in norm. If small shifts of every
function in a family are small uniformly in $\Lp$, then the family behaves like
an equicontinuous family after ignoring pointwise representatives. This is the
``almost continuity'' behind the Kolmogorov--Riesz theorem.

The purpose of this paper is to make that idea precise. We state the theorem in
$\Lp(\R^n)$, explain the translation condition, and outline how it is used to
prove relative compactness. The proof shows that boundedness controls vertical
size, tightness prevents mass from escaping to infinity, and almost continuity
allows approximation by smooth functions on compact sets.

\section{Preliminaries}

Let $1 \leq p < \infty$ and let $\mathcal{F}\subset \Lp(\R^n)$. For
$h\in\R^n$, define the translation operator
\[
  \tau_h f(x)=f(x+h).
\]
Translations are isometries on $\Lp(\R^n)$:
\[
  \norm{\tau_h f}_{p}=\norm{f}_{p}.
\]
For each fixed $f\in \Lp(\R^n)$, it is standard that
\[
  \lim_{h\to 0}\norm{\tau_h f-f}_{p}=0.
\]
The compactness theorem requires this convergence to be uniform for a whole
family.

\begin{definition}[Uniform almost continuity]
Let $1\leq p<\infty$. A family $\mathcal{F}\subset \Lp(\R^n)$ is uniformly
almost continuous if
\[
  \lim_{h\to 0}\sup_{f\in\mathcal{F}}\norm{\tau_h f-f}_{p}=0.
\]
\end{definition}

This definition is also called uniform translation continuity. It is analogous
to equicontinuity in the Arzela--Ascoli theorem, but it uses the $\Lp$ norm
instead of pointwise distances.

\begin{definition}[Tightness]
A family $\mathcal{F}\subset \Lp(\R^n)$ is tight if for every $\varepsilon>0$
there exists $R>0$ such that
\[
  \int_{\abs{x}>R}\abs{f(x)}^p\,dx < \varepsilon^p
  \quad\text{for every }f\in\mathcal{F}.
\]
\end{definition}

Tightness rules out the possibility that a sequence moves its mass farther and
farther away. For example, $f_k(x)=f(x-k)$ may be bounded and translation
continuous for each fixed function, but the set $\{f_k:k\in\mathbb{N}\}$ is not
compact in $\Lp(\R)$ because its mass escapes to infinity.

\section{The Kolmogorov--Riesz Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Kolmogorov--Riesz compactness theorem]
Let $1\leq p<\infty$ and let $\mathcal{F}\subset \Lp(\R^n)$. Then
$\mathcal{F}$ is relatively compact in $\Lp(\R^n)$ if and only if the following
three conditions hold:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item $\mathcal{F}$ is bounded in $\Lp(\R^n)$;
  \item $\mathcal{F}$ is tight;
  \item $\mathcal{F}$ is uniformly almost continuous, meaning
  \[
    \lim_{h\to 0}\sup_{f\in\mathcal{F}}\norm{\tau_h f-f}_{p}=0.
  \]
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

On a bounded domain, the tightness condition is usually replaced by a boundary
condition or becomes automatic after extending functions by zero and requiring
control near the boundary. On the whole space $\R^n$, tightness is essential.

\section{Why the Translation Condition Means Almost Continuity}

For a single function $f\in\Lp(\R^n)$, translation continuity says that $f$ does
not change much in average size when the input is shifted slightly. The
Kolmogorov--Riesz theorem asks for the same behavior uniformly across all
functions in $\mathcal{F}$. This is exactly the compactness analogue of
equicontinuity.

The condition
\[
  \sup_{f\in\mathcal{F}}\norm{\tau_h f-f}_{p}<\varepsilon
\]
for all sufficiently small $h$ says that there is one scale of translation that
works for the entire family. Thus the family has no arbitrarily sharp spikes,
oscillations, or jumps that appear at smaller and smaller scales.

\begin{proposition}
If $\mathcal{F}\subset \Lp(\R^n)$ is relatively compact, then $\mathcal{F}$ is
uniformly almost continuous.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $\varepsilon>0$. Since $\mathcal{F}$ is relatively compact, there exist
$g_1,\dots,g_m\in\Lp(\R^n)$ such that for every $f\in\mathcal{F}$ there is
$j$ with
\[
  \norm{f-g_j}_p<\varepsilon/3.
\]
For each fixed $g_j$, translation continuity gives a number $\delta_j>0$ such
that $\abs{h}<\delta_j$ implies
\[
  \norm{\tau_h g_j-g_j}_p<\varepsilon/3.
\]
Let $\delta=\min_j\delta_j$. If $\abs{h}<\delta$ and $f\in\mathcal{F}$, choose
$g_j$ with $\norm{f-g_j}_p<\varepsilon/3$. Since translations are isometries,
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \norm{\tau_h f-f}_p
  &\leq \norm{\tau_h f-\tau_h g_j}_p
    +\norm{\tau_h g_j-g_j}_p
    +\norm{g_j-f}_p  \\
  &< \varepsilon.
\end{aligned}
\]
Taking the supremum over $f\in\mathcal{F}$ proves uniform almost continuity.
\end{proof}

This proof is short but important. It shows that compactness forces a uniform
translation scale. The more difficult direction is that boundedness, tightness,
and almost continuity together imply compactness.

\section{How Almost Continuity Enters the Proof}

We sketch the main mechanism. Let $\rho$ be a standard smooth mollifier and set
$\rho_\eta(x)=\eta^{-n}\rho(x/\eta)$. For $f\in\Lp(\R^n)$ define
\[
  f_\eta=\rho_\eta*f.
\]
Mollification averages the values of $f$ over a small ball. The translation
condition controls the error introduced by this averaging:
\[
\begin{aligned}
  \norm{\rho_\eta*f-f}_p
  &=
  \left\|
    \int_{\R^n}\rho_\eta(y)\bigl(\tau_{-y}f-f\bigr)\,dy
  \right\|_p \\
  &\leq
  \int_{\R^n}\rho_\eta(y)\norm{\tau_{-y}f-f}_p\,dy.
\end{aligned}
\]
If $\eta$ is small, then $y$ is small on the support of $\rho_\eta$, and uniform
almost continuity makes the last integral small uniformly for
$f\in\mathcal{F}$. Therefore
\[
  \sup_{f\in\mathcal{F}}\norm{\rho_\eta*f-f}_p
\]
can be made arbitrarily small.

Now tightness allows us to restrict attention to a large ball $B_R$. On $B_R$,
the mollified functions are smoother and have uniform derivative bounds
depending on $\eta$ and the $\Lp$ bound of $\mathcal{F}$. Smoothness gives
finite-dimensional approximation: one may approximate the functions by their
values, averages, or polynomial approximations on a sufficiently fine partition
of $B_R$.

Thus the proof has three approximation steps:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
  \item use tightness to ignore the tail outside a large ball;
  \item use almost continuity to replace each $f$ by a mollified function
  $\rho_\eta*f$;
  \item use smoothness on the compact ball to approximate the mollified family
  by finitely many parameters.
\end{enumerate}
These steps produce a finite $\varepsilon$-net for $\mathcal{F}$ in $\Lp$,
which is exactly total boundedness. Since $\Lp(\R^n)$ is complete, total
boundedness is equivalent to relative compactness.

\section{A Useful Translation Criterion}

The translation condition often appears in applications through estimates on
gradients.

\begin{proposition}
Let $\Omega\subset\R^n$ be bounded and let
$\mathcal{F}\subset W^{1,p}(\Omega)$ be bounded. After extending functions to
$\R^n$ in a controlled way, $\mathcal{F}$ is uniformly almost continuous in
$\Lp$ on compact subsets of $\Omega$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For a smooth function $f$ and small $h$, the fundamental theorem of calculus
gives
\[
  f(x+h)-f(x)=\int_0^1 h\cdot \nabla f(x+th)\,dt.
\]
Taking $\Lp$ norms and using Minkowski's inequality,
\[
  \norm{\tau_h f-f}_p
  \leq \abs{h}\,\norm{\nabla f}_p.
\]
The same estimate follows for Sobolev functions by approximation. If
$\norm{\nabla f}_p$ is uniformly bounded over $\mathcal{F}$, then the right-hand
side tends to zero uniformly as $h\to0$.
\end{proof}

This proposition explains why the Kolmogorov--Riesz theorem is closely related
to compact Sobolev embeddings. A derivative bound prevents rapid oscillation,
and this prevention is measured exactly by translation continuity.

\section{Conclusion}

The translation condition in the Kolmogorov--Riesz theorem is best understood
as almost continuity in the $\Lp$ norm. It replaces pointwise equicontinuity
with a condition that makes sense for equivalence classes of functions. Together
with boundedness and tightness, it gives a complete compactness criterion in
$\Lp(\R^n)$.

The theorem is powerful because it separates the causes of non-compactness:
unbounded size, escape of mass to infinity, and oscillation at arbitrarily small
scales. The almost continuity condition rules out the third problem. In this
way, it is the analytic heart of the compactness mechanism.
